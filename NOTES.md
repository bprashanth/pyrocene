Ok let me add some interesting elements to this game then you can generate the rule book. 

1. The game is cooperative with two roles: ecologist and ranger. Against the ai. 
2. The ais role is not to be overly realistic but to teach the players. We want to teach them: a. Why a ranger is so action focused b. Why we also need observation and learning c. The most stable ecosystems are ones that can tolerate the disturbance (in this case wildfire) and recover, not ones that try to prevent the distrubance or supress it reactively. 
3. There are some salient things players learn via the game. What sources of data the ecologist has and how to think like one. What options the ranger has and how to think like one. And the random and disastrous effects of nature's fury when left unbalanced. There should be a bounded set of each of these, so it seems manageable for a player to figure the options and limitations out in a few minutes or via playing.
4. The baord is generated as a set of squares. Each square has a few variables which are hidden to the user but exposed to the ai. The variables need to be discovered by the players.
5. The ecologist is the learning player. The ranger is the action player. Every night the ai simulates something to teach the players. There is an ecological health score. This score is affected by wildfires alone - this has a spilling effect on the ecology - for example it can destroy a village and that would be a high cost. If the fire is contained eg by a fire breaks put in by a ranger, it increases eco health. If there is a large fire that destroys the native patch is lowers. Since the cost to humans is highest, ranger is often forced to take supression actions but must quickly realize this will just exhaust money. For example if they keep relocating teams or retraining communities or removing invasives blindly they'll just keep coming up or the fire will happen elsewhere. 
6. Apart from the standard data sources like satellite and drone and forest survey the ecologist can learn from there is an odd one, community qualitative. The community can say eg those 5 squares in different parts of the board always have fires (vague) in which case the ecologist must understand what is the common feature among this 5. Or they may say all windy nights there's fires or something like that which is basically a clue that direct sensing and observation doesn't provide. In that sense it is like a wildcard. Sometimes (rarely) they may use dialects we don't underatan in which case it's a waste. 
7. There is one quirk more around communication. Every turn the ecologist can place a flag for danger and/or remove one based on updates sensors. But they can't declare why the place is a risk. To determine why, the ranger must sink a turn in communicating with the ecologist. They may then disclose that square e7 is risky because of fuel (dead leaves trees etc) or invasives. 
8. There is one more ability the ecologist has which is hindcast. They get to see the precise history of a given square when used. Whether it had fires. What natives or invasives grew there. This is specifically useful because if a square is termed risky due to invasives the ecologist can spend another turn in hindcast and tell the ranger what to plant there. Then in two turns the ranger can dramatically improve eco score via anr restoration. 

As you can see time is very sped up in this environment and the game must stay engaging which means the ai must be focused on surfacing learnings and creating realistic simulations. Rather than simply trying to simulate nature the ai has to incite action and learning by creating scenarios via fire deployment. Winning is score staying above 90 for 3 terms (resilience) losing is the score going below 50.

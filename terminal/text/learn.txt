# --- the two ends of the same instrument ------------------------------------
[intro.p1.title]
A fire the size of a room

[intro.p1.body]
Fire is cheapest to stop in its first minutes. The satellites watching for it could only see a blaze once it was already big, and by then the cheap options are gone.

FireSat was built for the other end of that problem. Its sensors can pick out a fire about five metres across. Three of them went up in July 2026.

[intro.p1.source]
Google Research and the Earth Fire Alliance

[intro.p2.title]
Eight thousand fires at once

[intro.p2.body]
Now zoom the other way. In June 2013 the haze over Sumatra closed schools in Singapore and Malaysia. Everyone could see the smoke. Nobody could say where it came from.

Satellites mapped more than 8,000 hotspots in Riau that month. Laid over a map of who held which land, nearly 40 percent sat inside pulpwood or oil palm concessions, and 88 percent were burning on peat.

[intro.p2.source]
Eyes on the Forest and WWF Indonesia, 2013

# --- seeing is not enough ----------------------------------------------------
[intro.p3.title]
Seeing is not stopping

[intro.p3.body]
Spotting a fire is not the same as stopping one. Somebody still has to get there.

In Gadchiroli, Farmers for Forests watch 4,000 hectares with drones. In one dry season they mapped 282 fires and reached them in 51 minutes on average. Their fastest was five.

[intro.p3.source]
Farmers for Forests, Gadchiroli

[intro.p4.title]
Better still, before it starts

[intro.p4.body]
But what if the fire never started? That takes understanding it instead of chasing it.

Not all fire is bad. These forests have burned gently for centuries, and people used it to keep them open. What turns a good fire bad is fuel. Invasive plants pile up fuel where there was none, and a fire that would have crept along the ground takes the canopy instead.

[intro.p4.source]
Keystone Foundation, on forest fire ecology in India

# --- the handover ------------------------------------------------------------
[intro.p5.title]
Your job

[intro.p5.body]
This game puts you in the chair where those calls get made. One action a night, on land that keeps moving whether you act or not.

Look, so you know what is actually out there. Remove the lantana that is building the fuel. Restore what you clear, before it is taken back.

Hold the forest above the line for three nights and it is yours.
